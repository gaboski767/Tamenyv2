Most citizens wait until symptoms are severe before seeking care. By then, treatment windows narrow and costs rise dramatically.

    Cancer Screenings

    Breast, Cervical, Prostate, Colorectal, Lung

    5 protocols

    Breast Cancer

    Women at moderate or high familial risk
    Risk-based
    View details
    WhoWomen at moderate/high familial risk. High-risk: ages 30–39 (e.g. BRCA mutation). Moderate-risk: ages 50–59.
    MethodMRI scan (younger, high-risk women) or Mammography. NHS Programme invites women 50–70 every 3 years. Women over 70 can self-refer.
    FrequencyAnnual for higher-risk women. Every 3 years under NHS Population Programme (50–70).
    Women over 70 may self-refer every 3 years via the NHS programme even when not routinely invited.

    Cervical Cancer (HPV)

    Women & individuals with a cervix, 25–64
    All Women
    View details
    WhoAll women and individuals with a cervix aged 25–64.
    MethodPrimary HPV testing using liquid-based cytology (LBC). Cervical cell sample → tested for high-risk HPV (hrHPV). If positive → cytology performed.
    FrequencyAges 25–49: every 3 years. Ages 50–64: every 5 years.
    Over 65: not routinely screened unless not screened since age 50 or recent abnormal results.

    Prostate Cancer (PSA)

    Men 50+, or 45+ with higher risk
    Age/Risk-based
    View details
    WhoMen 50+. Men 45+ if higher risk: Black African/Caribbean ancestry or first-degree family history (father/brother).
    MethodPSA (Prostate Specific Antigen) blood test.
    FrequencyTypically every 1–2 years depending on results and risk level.
    Borderline results: repeat PSA testing or MRI for further evaluation.

    Colorectal Cancer (FIT)

    Adults 50–74 at average risk
    All Adults
    Home Test Available
    View details
    WhoAdults 50–74 years at average risk.
    MethodFaecal Immunochemical Test (FIT) — detects hidden blood in stool. Home-based, non-invasive.
    FrequencyEvery 2 years.
    Positive FIT → colonoscopy to detect colorectal cancer or advanced polyps.

    Lung Cancer (Low-dose CT)

    High-risk individuals 55–74, smokers
    High Risk Only
    View details
    WhoHigh-risk individuals identified through GP records. Age 55–74, current or former smokers.
    MethodLow-dose CT scan — initial lung health check appointment.
    FrequencyInitial screen; if normal → repeat every 2 years.
    If abnormality: 3-month or 12-month surveillance scans, or immediate referral if cancer suspected.

    Cardiovascular & Blood Vessels

    Hypertension, CVD Risk, Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm

    3 protocols

    Hypertension Screening

    All adults — includes annual check for T2DM
    All Adults
    Home Test Available
    View details
    WhoAll adults. Annual checks if you have Type 2 Diabetes.
    MethodBlood pressure measurement — clinic or validated home device.
    FrequencyEvery 5 years if BP <140/90 mmHg. Annually if high-normal readings or Type 2 Diabetes.
    Early detection prevents cardiovascular complications. BP ≥140/90 mmHg on repeat = hypertension diagnosis.

    CVD Risk Assessment

    Adults 40–74 without existing CVD
    All Adults 40+
    View details
    WhoAdults 40–74 without known CVD. Excludes those with existing CVD, Type 1 Diabetes, CKD Stage 3–5, or familial hypercholesterolaemia.
    MethodQRISK3 calculator using electronic health records data.
    FrequencyEvery 5 years.
    ≥10% 10-year risk: consider statins and intervention. <10%: lifestyle advice given.

    AAA (Aortic Aneurysm)

    Men aged 65 — one-time NHS programme
    Men, Age 65
    View details
    WhoMen aged 65. One-time NHS programme invitation.
    MethodAbdominal ultrasound scan to measure the diameter of the abdominal aorta.
    FrequencyOnce at age 65.
    Follow-up by size: <3 cm = no aneurysm; 3.0–4.4 cm = 12-monthly ultrasound; 4.5–5.4 cm = 3-monthly; ≥5.5 cm = vascular surgery referral.

    Metabolic & Diabetes

    Type 2 Diabetes, Diabetic Retinopathy

    2 protocols

    Type 2 Diabetes

    Adults 40–74, two-stage process
    All Adults 40+
    View details
    WhoAdults 40–74. Two-stage process.
    MethodStage 1: Risk assessment using QDiabetes or Leicester Risk Score (NHS Health Checks). Stage 2 (high-risk): HbA1c blood test or fasting plasma glucose.
    FrequencyLow risk: every 5 years. Moderate risk: every 3 years. High risk / prediabetes: annually.
    Prediabetes = HbA1c 42–47 mmol/mol. Full diabetes ≥48 mmol/mol on repeat testing.

    Diabetic Retinopathy

    All diabetic patients aged 12+
    Diabetes Only
    View details
    WhoIndividuals with Type 1 or Type 2 Diabetes aged 12 years and older.
    MethodDigital retinal photography after pupil dilation — detects retinal damage caused by diabetes.
    FrequencyAnnually.
    Early detection prevents blindness — diabetic retinopathy is the leading cause of preventable blindness in working-age adults.

    Kidney & Liver

    CKD, Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC), NAFLD Fibrosis

    3 protocols

    Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

    Risk groups — not population-wide screening
    Risk Groups
    View details
    WhoRisk groups: Diabetes, Hypertension, Cardiovascular disease, Structural kidney disease, Systemic diseases (e.g. SLE), Gout, Family history of ESRD, Persistent haematuria or proteinuria.
    MethodBlood test: eGFR (creatinine-based). Urine test: Albumin-Creatinine Ratio (ACR).
    FrequencyDepends on underlying condition and clinical risk.
    Special monitoring: at least 3 years after Acute Kidney Injury, and for those on long-term nephrotoxic drugs (NSAIDs, lithium).

    Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)

    Cirrhosis or chronic Hepatitis B patients
    High Risk
    View details
    WhoPatients with cirrhosis. Individuals with chronic Hepatitis B infection.
    MethodLiver ultrasound scan. Optional: Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) blood test as complement.
    FrequencyEvery 6 months.
    Surveillance identifies early-stage HCC before symptoms — when curative treatment is still possible.

    Liver Fibrosis in NAFLD

    Patients with Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease
    NAFLD Patients
    View details
    WhoPatients diagnosed with Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD).
    MethodEnhanced Liver Fibrosis (ELF) blood test — a validated serum panel that identifies fibrosis stage.
    FrequencyEvery 3 years.
    Identifies patients progressing toward cirrhosis before irreversible damage — enabling early lifestyle or pharmacological intervention.

    Gastrointestinal & IBS

    H. pylori, Colorectal (FIT), Coeliac, IBD / Crohn's, IBS, Gastric Cancer

    5 protocols

    Colorectal Cancer (FIT)

    Adults 50–74 — every 2 years
    All Adults 50+
    Home Test Available
    View details
    WhoAdults 50–74 at average risk. From age 40 if first-degree family history of colorectal cancer.
    MethodFaecal Immunochemical Test (FIT) — detects hidden blood in stool. Home-based stool sample kit, non-invasive. Positive result → colonoscopy referral.
    FrequencyEvery 2 years.
    Home testing: Stool kit posted to your door. Collected at home, returned by post or at a partner lab. Results within 48 hours.

    H. pylori (Helicobacter pylori)

    Symptomatic adults or dyspepsia patients
    Symptomatic / Risk
    Home Test Available
    View details
    WhoAdults with persistent dyspepsia, epigastric pain, or chronic bloating. First-line test before endoscopy in under-55s without alarm features.
    MethodStool antigen test (preferred non-invasive) or urea breath test. Both available as home or clinic-based tests.
    FrequencyTest-and-treat at presentation; confirm eradication 4–6 weeks after treatment completion.
    High prevalence in Egypt: H. pylori infects ~70–80% of Egyptian adults and is a major cause of peptic ulcers and gastric cancer. Early test-and-treat is recommended.

    Coeliac Disease

    Symptomatic adults; first-degree relatives of coeliac patients
    Symptomatic / Family
    View details
    WhoAdults with unexplained anaemia, chronic diarrhoea, bloating, weight loss, or fatigue. First-degree relatives of confirmed coeliac patients.
    MethodBlood test: IgA tissue transglutaminase antibodies (tTG-IgA) + total IgA level. Must be eating gluten at time of testing. Positive serology → duodenal biopsy for confirmation.
    FrequencyOnce on clinical suspicion. Repeat if symptoms recur and seronegative (check IgA deficiency).
    Do not start a gluten-free diet before testing — this produces false-negative results.

    IBD / Crohn's / Ulcerative Colitis

    Adults with chronic diarrhoea, bleeding, or abdominal pain
    Symptomatic
    View details
    WhoAdults with persistent diarrhoea (>6 weeks), rectal bleeding, abdominal cramps, unintentional weight loss, or a first-degree relative with IBD.
    MethodStage 1: Faecal calprotectin (stool test — differentiates inflammatory from functional disease). Stage 2: Colonoscopy with biopsy for confirmed IBD diagnosis.
    FrequencyOn clinical suspicion. Ongoing surveillance colonoscopy in established IBD every 1–5 years depending on extent and risk.
    Faecal calprotectin >50 μg/g distinguishes IBD from IBS with high sensitivity — a key non-invasive first-line test.

    IBS Assessment & Exclusion

    Adults with chronic functional gut symptoms
    GIT
    View details
    WhoAdults with ≥6 months of abdominal pain, bloating, and altered bowel habits (diarrhoea-predominant, constipation-predominant, or mixed). Diagnosis requires exclusion of organic disease.
    MethodExclude: CBC (anaemia), CRP/ESR (inflammation), coeliac serology (tTG-IgA), faecal calprotectin (IBD). Positive alarm features → colonoscopy. Rome IV criteria applied.
    FrequencyInitial work-up at presentation. Re-evaluate if alarm features develop (rectal bleeding, weight loss, nocturnal symptoms, age onset >50).
    IBS is a diagnosis of exclusion. The exclusion panel (above) can largely be completed with home stool tests + a blood draw at a partner lab.

    Pregnancy Screenings

    Gestational Diabetes, Pre-eclampsia, GBS, Down Syndrome, Fetal Anomaly, Infections

    6 protocols

    Gestational Diabetes

    Pregnant women with risk factors
    Pregnancy
    View details
    WhoPregnant women with: BMI ≥30, previous gestational diabetes, previous baby ≥4.5 kg, first-degree family history of diabetes, or high-risk ethnic background.
    Timing24–28 weeks gestation. Earlier if previous gestational diabetes.
    MethodOral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT): overnight fast → fasting blood glucose → 75g glucose drink → repeat blood test at 2 hours.
    Diagnostic: Fasting glucose ≥5.6 mmol/L or 2-hour glucose ≥7.8 mmol/L confirms gestational diabetes.

    Pre-eclampsia Monitoring

    All pregnant women — every antenatal visit
    All Pregnancies
    View details
    MethodBlood pressure measurement and urine dipstick test for protein at every antenatal visit.
    FrequencyEvery antenatal visit throughout pregnancy.
    Warning signs: BP ≥140/90 mmHg and/or proteinuria — requires urgent clinical review.

    Group B Streptococcus (GBS)

    Risk-based approach — not universal
    Risk-based
    View details
    WhoHigh-risk women: previous baby with GBS, GBS detected earlier in current pregnancy, fever during labour, or premature labour.
    MethodVaginal and rectal swabs followed by laboratory bacterial culture — if clinically indicated.
    NICE: risk-based approach, not universal screening. Prevention: intrapartum antibiotic prophylaxis for high-risk women.

    Down Syndrome Screening

    All pregnant women, 10–14 weeks
    All Pregnancies
    View details
    Timing10–14 weeks of pregnancy.
    MethodCombined test: ultrasound measurement of nuchal translucency + maternal blood test measuring PAPP-A and free β-hCG.
    If increased risk: offered NIPT, or diagnostic testing — chorionic villus sampling (CVS) or amniocentesis.

    Fetal Anomaly Scan

    All pregnant women, 18–21 weeks
    All Pregnancies
    View details
    Timing18–21 weeks of pregnancy.
    MethodDetailed ultrasound scan examining fetal anatomy.
    Conditions assessed: brain, spine, heart, kidneys, limbs, and abdominal organs — structural abnormalities.

    Infectious Diseases in Pregnancy

    All pregnant women — booking appointment
    All Pregnancies
    View details
    MethodBlood tests at booking appointment: HIV, Hepatitis B, Syphilis. Rubella immunity may also be assessed.
    Early detection allows interventions to prevent mother-to-child transmission.

    Frequently Asked Questions
    Do I need a doctor's referral?
    No. Tameny operates as a direct-access preventive screening platform. You do not need a doctor's referral to book any of our packages.

    How fast will I get results?
    Standard results are delivered within 48 hours. For urgent screenings, a 24-hour priority option is available at partner labs.
    Can I get home sample collection?
    Yes. All Intermediate and Comprehensive packages include the option of a certified nurse home visit for sample collection within Alexandria.
    Are female practitioners available?
    Absolutely. All partner labs marked with the blue "Female Staff ✓" badge guarantee a female practitioner for all sample collection procedures on request.
